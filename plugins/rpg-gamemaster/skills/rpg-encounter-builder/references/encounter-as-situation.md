# Encounter as Situation

> **Examples are illustrative — never reuse verbatim.** All examples in this file use generic parties and generic settings, or clearly-public published media (e.g. Critical Role's *Vox Machina*). No private campaign content appears here. Copying an example into a real campaign session produces unrelated flavor.

---

## Why a fight is a situation, not a bag of HP

A balanced XP budget tells the GM whether the encounter is survivable. It does not tell the players what to *do* — other than wait until the last enemy falls.

A **situation** is an unstable configuration of forces with multiple possible outcomes. A bag of HP has one outcome: somebody dies until the other side runs out. A situation has exits, stakes, decisions. The difference between "kill the guards in the warehouse" and "reach the hostage before the fire spreads, while the guards try to stop you" is the difference between a combat and a scene.

Every encounter this skill builds is a situation. The math layer makes it survivable. The situation layer makes it worth running.

---

## The five elements of an encounter-as-situation

### 1. An objective beyond "win"

The players should be able to answer "what are we trying to accomplish here?" with something other than "survive." Objectives create decisions during the fight, not just between fights.

**Useful objective types:**
- **Escort or protect** — keep an NPC alive; hold a position; get someone to the exit. The fight becomes a race against the enemies.
- **Retrieve** — grab the item, the document, the hostage, the body. The moment of retrieval changes the fight's calculus.
- **Delay** — hold the line for N rounds; stall the ritual until the clock runs out; buy time for the others to act.
- **Destroy** — not the enemies, but the thing (the machine, the portal, the summoning circle). The enemies are the obstacle, not the objective.
- **Survive and escape** — get out with the information; retreat with casualties the campaign can absorb. Retreat is a valid outcome.
- **Expose without fighting** — gather evidence, identify the ringleader, tag the leader for later. Sometimes the objective is information, not victory.

The objective does not need to be announced to the players. It emerges from the situation. But the GM must know what it is, because it determines when the fight is "won" — even if the enemies are still standing.

### 2. A terrain feature that changes tactics

An encounter with no terrain is a fight in a void. One terrain feature that *does something* changes the whole dynamic.

**Features that change fights:**
- **Something that limits movement** — a choke point, a wall of fire, a frozen river with cracking ice, collapsed scaffolding. Forces decisions about positioning.
- **Something that provides cover** — market stalls, overturned carts, pillars. Changes ranged vs. melee calculus. Ambushes become possible.
- **Something that can be used as a weapon** — a chandelier that falls, barrels that can be pushed, a lever that opens a portcullis. Rewards environmental creativity.
- **Something with a clock** — a burning building's failing beams, a rising flood, a ritual that completes at the end of round 3. Adds pressure that isn't another enemy.
- **A height differential** — a balcony, a cliff edge, a fighting retreat up a staircase. Changes who has advantage and who is exposed.
- **A door or gate** — the thing the enemies are defending, or the thing the players need to reach. Simple but effective.

One feature is enough for a Compact encounter. Two to three features, with interactions between them, is enough for a set-piece fight.

### 3. The reason the fight exists

An encounter that exists for no in-world reason is a random encounter. Every encounter in a prepared session connects to something larger: a front, a quest, a revelation, a relationship.

**Questions that establish the reason:**
- *Why are these enemies here, in this place, at this moment?* The answer should be a sentence that ties to an existing campaign element — the faction's plan, the portent that fired, the thing they were guarding, the orders they received.
- *What happens in the world if the players win? If they lose? If they run?* The answer should change something in the fiction — not just a reward, but a shift in the campaign state.
- *Do the enemies want something other than "kill the PCs"?* Enemies with goals (defending a package, buying time for their employer to escape, retrieving the key before the players can use it) behave differently from enemies whose only goal is combat.

Tie the reason to the vault's content: a faction's name, a front's portent, a quest stage, a revelation ID from the clue map. This is what makes the encounter feel like part of the campaign rather than an interruption of it.

### 4. Escalation — what changes mid-fight

The fight's opening position is not its only position. Something changes. Escalation keeps the encounter from playing out the same way every time.

**Escalation types:**
- **Reinforcements** — more enemies arrive after N rounds, or when a specific condition triggers (an enemy uses an action to call for help; the bell rings; the portcullis opens). Not a guarantee — a possibility the GM deploys if the fight is going too easily.
- **A clock** — something in the environment completes or fails on a countdown (the fire reaches the support beams on round 3; the ritual finishes if uninterrupted). Creates urgency without adding enemies.
- **A turn** — one of the enemies defects, flees, or changes sides when reduced to bloodied (half HP). Introduces uncertainty.
- **A new objective** — a development mid-fight changes what the players are trying to do (the person they're protecting runs for it; a third party intervenes; the ringleader tries to escape).

Not every encounter needs escalation. Trivial and Low encounters can stay flat. Moderate and above benefit from at least one escalation trigger. Severe and Extreme encounters should have escalation as a default.

### 5. The exit — when and how it ends

A fight that can only end by killing every enemy is a fight with no decisions. Build in exits.

**Exit types:**
- **Morale** — enemies flee or surrender when reduced below a certain number (half their side falls; the leader goes down; the objective is lost). Common in creatures with survival instincts; rare in undead or constructs. Define the trigger and what it looks like in fiction.
- **Objective achieved** — the fight ends when the players accomplish the objective, even if enemies remain (they escape with the hostage; the ritual is interrupted; the bridge collapses). The remaining enemies are not a problem.
- **Negotiation** — enemies are willing to deal (stop fighting in exchange for information, a hostage, safe passage). This is an exit only if the GM has decided what the enemies would accept. Define it before the fight starts.
- **Escalation exit** — the players escape instead of winning. They got the item; they get out. The enemies hold the field but didn't stop the objective.

Name the exit condition in the encounter's situation block. The GM decides at the table whether to use it — but knowing it exists changes how the fight is paced.

## The situation block in the output

Every encounter this skill produces ends with a situation block before the Persist section. The block answers five questions in plain sentences:

```
Objective:   [what the players are trying to accomplish beyond "win"]
Terrain:     [one or two features that change how the fight plays out]
Reason:      [why this fight exists in the campaign — tied to a front/quest/revelation]
Escalation:  [what changes mid-fight, and the trigger]
Exit:        [how the fight ends — morale threshold, objective achieved, negotiation point]
```

Keep it short. The situation block is a GM prompt, not a scene description. Two to four sentences per field is enough; more belongs in the Full output's expanded encounter reasoning.
