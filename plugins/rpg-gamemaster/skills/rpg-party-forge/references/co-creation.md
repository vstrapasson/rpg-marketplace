# Co-Creation (party-forge — session zero)

> Operational copy of the kit's shared DNA (master: `design-philosophy.md` at the kit root). This is the **front-end** of the skill — run it before building any PC. The skill is a creative partner that draws the player's character out, sharpens it, and ties it into the world; it is **not** a character vending machine.

> **About the examples here:** illustrative only — generic, or from public media (e.g. Critical Role's *Vox Machina*). **Never reuse them verbatim** — they are teaching aids; copying them would bleed unrelated flavor into the table's game.

> **The boundary that shapes this whole intake:** a PC is **co-owned**. Unlike an NPC, the player has final say on who their character is. Run the shared layer *with* the player; the player decides; the GM layers the secret on privately afterward. Spark, reflect, offer — never overwrite a stated choice with a "better" one.

---

## Part A — Set up the party (run once, before any character)

Before the first PC, settle the table-level decisions. These are inherited or agreed once and apply to every character.

1. **Inherit tone and truth.** Read `campaign-bible-*.md`: the central truth, the antagonist, the thematic pillars, the tone level. The PCs live in *this* world at *this* register — don't reset it. The bible's central truth is also the well you draw each PC's GM-secret from (Part C of `pc-frameworks.md`).
2. **Confirm the system.** PF2e by default. The skill captures the build the player made; it does not optimize it.
3. **Capture party level and size.** Ask once: what level does the party start at, and how many characters? These become canonical — written to the party overview so the encounter and embate builders stop asking (`party-composition.md`).
4. **Agree the lines and veils.** Session zero is the right place for the safety conversation: hard lines (never on screen) and veils (happens, not described). A dark-leaning campaign earns trust by naming its limits. Record them in the party overview.
5. **Name the central pull.** One sentence on why *this* party is together and what's pulling them into the campaign — the shared situation the individual hooks will each tie into.

State the **escape hatch early**: "At any point say 'just run with it' and I'll draft from what we have, then we tune." A player must never feel trapped in the interview.

## Part B — The per-PC loop

Repeat per character. The engine is in `pc-frameworks.md`; this is how you draw it out.

1. **Open.** Pull *their* picture first, from the inside. "Before a name or a class — what does your character *believe* about the world, and what happened to make them believe it?" The belief and the wound are the core; lead there, not with ancestry or stats.
2. **Spark when stuck.** If the player has no answer, offer 2–4 concrete, evocative options to pick or remix (use AskUserQuestion). Options are launchpads to react against, not a menu to settle for.
3. **Reflect.** Play the answer back, sharper. "So she's less 'a soldier who lost her unit' and more 'a soldier who gave the order that got them killed, and now can't take orders or give them' — yes?"
4. **Probe the gaps and the anchor.** The probes specific to *this* skill:
   - *"What pulls your character into this campaign — is there a faction, a place, or a person from the bible they're already tangled with?"* (the hook — every PC needs one)
   - *"What does your character want out loud — and what do they actually need but wouldn't admit?"* (want vs. need)
   - *"How do they act at the table under pressure — and with the rest of the party?"* (voice)
5. **Build by section, check in.** Draft one section, show it, invite course-correction before the next. Don't dump the whole sheet mid-collaboration. The player approves as you go.

### The load-bearing decisions for a PC (ask about these; default the rest)

In priority order — the **core** is the engine everything grows from:

1. **The core — belief + wound.** What the character believes, and the specific, active experience that created it. Spend the most elicitation here.
2. **The campaign hook.** Which faction / region / location / front / clue does the wound or want pull them toward? Name it by exact vault name. (This is the load-bearing decision the NPC creator doesn't have — a PC must anchor into the world.)
3. **Want vs. need + the table voice.** The conscious goal, the unadmitted need, and a performable way they show up at the table.
4. **The build (capture, don't optimize).** Ancestry, class, background — whatever the player made (Pathbuilder, by hand). Record it; don't re-spec it.

**Default freely:** the exact name spelling, fine appearance detail, secondary relationships, the precise read-aloud wording — unless the player signals they care.

### The test for "am I interrogating?"

Open questions + sparks + reflection, one thread at a time, always moving the character forward. **If you've asked two questions without giving something back** (a reflection, an option, a drafted beat), you've slipped into interrogation. *Give before you ask again.*

### Worked micro-example (drawing out the core + the anchor — generic)

*(Do not reuse verbatim.)*

> **You:** "Before a class or a name — what does your character *believe* about the world, and what happened to make them believe it?"
> **Player:** "He's a goblin. He believes loyalty is the only thing worth anything. His whole crew got wiped out before the campaign starts."
> **You (reflect + spark):** "So loyalty is sacred *because* he's seen what its absence costs — and he survived the thing that killed everyone he was loyal to. That survival is the wound: he's the one who lived. Quick fork that sharpens it — was he spared by luck (out on a job that night), or did he run? One makes him grieving, the other makes him guilty. Which bites more for you?"
> **Player:** "He was out on a job. Pure luck."
> **You (reflect + anchor):** "Grief, then, not guilt — clean to play. Now the hook: that wiped-out crew was tied to *something* in this city. The bible has the Mão Funda running relics through the underlayers. Was his crew part of that network, or a rival it crossed? Either way, his hunt for who did it points him straight at a faction we already have on the board — that's his thread into the campaign."

## Part C — Read-aloud & voice (the table layer)

A PC's read-aloud is the player's, not the GM's — but the skill can offer two useful beats for the sheet:

- **The table voice line** — one quoted line in the character's voice, the 2-second grab the player (and GM) can reach for. Doubles as the voice hook.
- **The "enters session one" beat** — a short framing of how this character walks into the first scene, so session one has a clean on-ramp for them. Mark it as an *offering*, not a script — the player drives their own character.

Keep them short (~40–70 words), sensory, and open-ended. Never narrate what the character *feels* or *decides* — that's the player's to play. Mark blocks as a blockquote labelled **Read-aloud** (PT-BR: **Para ler à mesa**); match the table's language.

### Example (a "enters session one" beat, ~50 words — generic)

> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *The job said the back room of the Drowned Lamp, midnight, ask for the woman with the burned hand. Smoke, low talk, a card game that stops when the door opens. Somewhere under the floor, water moves. The woman with the burned hand looks up — and she already knows the name.*

*(Do not reuse verbatim. Adapt to the character and the campaign's opening.)*
