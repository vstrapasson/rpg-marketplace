# Co-Creation (rpg-critique)

> Operational copy of the kit's shared DNA (master: `design-philosophy.md` at the kit root). This is the **front-end** of the skill — run it before spawning anything. Convening a panel is expensive in time and tokens; five critics pointed at the wrong scope is five wasted reports. The interview exists to aim the room.

> **About the examples here:** illustrative only — generic, or from public media. **Never reuse them verbatim.**

> **One difference from every other skill in the kit:** this one critiques the user's own creative work. The elicitation is not just about scope — it is about aiming the panel at what the GM actually wants to get better at, and setting expectations honestly before the report lands.

## Part A — The scoping loop

Five things to draw out. Most are quick; the first two are load-bearing.

1. **What is under review** — which aspects: lore and arc, NPCs and factions, cities and places, conducting. Any subset. Default is all four *if* the material exists.
2. **How wide** — the whole campaign, one arc or act, the last N sessions, one region and its cast. Narrower is usually better; a panel with a sharp scope produces sharper fixes.
3. **What is worrying them** — the single most useful question in this interview, and the one to ask early. A GM usually has a suspicion ("I think my NPCs are flat", "sessions 2 and 3 dragged and I don't know why"). That suspicion should shape which persona gets the deepest brief — not by biasing the verdict, but by aiming the sample.
4. **Depth** — a focused pass (one aspect, one or two sessions) or a full campaign review. This drives how much the librarian samples.
5. **Whether to persist a summary** — the HTML report is always written; a short summary note in the vault is optional. Ask at the end, not the start.

### Read the room first

- **"Review my campaign"** with no other signal → propose a scope rather than asking four questions. Look at what exists (how many sessions, how many transcripts, how many artifacts), then offer two or three concrete scopes to pick from with AskUserQuestion. Giving a proposal to react against beats asking them to specify one.
- **A specific worry** ("my sessions feel railroaded") → aim narrow and say so. Note aloud that the whole panel still runs — the point of five lenses is that the diagnosis may not be where the symptom is.
- **"Just run it"** → default to the last two sessions with transcripts plus every artifact they touch, name that choice out loud, and go.
- **No transcripts in the vault** → say this before spending anything: conducting cannot be judged from prep. Offer the artifacts-only review (four aspects become three, O Jogador mostly abstains), and mention that recording future sessions is what unlocks the other half.

State the **escape hatch early**: "Say 'just run it' and I'll pick a scope, name my assumptions, and go."

### The load-bearing decisions (ask about these; default the rest)

1. **Scope** — which aspects, how wide. Wrong scope wastes the whole run.
2. **Which sessions count as the conducting evidence** — most recent, a specific one the GM remembers going badly, or a spread.
3. **Whether a summary gets persisted to the vault** — ask at the end.

**Default freely:** which personas run (all five, always — a panel with seats removed stops being a panel), the model spread, the report filename, whether critiques are archived alongside the report. Do not ask about any of these unless the user raises them.

### Set expectations before the room convenes

Say this once, in your own words, before spawning — it is the difference between a report that helps and a report that stings:

- It is **five expert perspectives**, not a score. Different lenses, deliberately different opinions.
- They **will disagree**, and the disagreements are kept rather than resolved — they are usually the most useful part.
- Every claim is **anchored to a quote** from the vault or the transcripts. If something cannot be evidenced, it gets marked as such instead of guessed.
- It judges the campaign **against its own stated intent**, not against a generic ideal.

### The test for "am I interrogating?"

Open question, then give something back — a proposed scope, a count of what exists, a named assumption. **If you have asked two questions without giving something back, you have slipped into interrogation.** *Give before you ask again.*

### Worked micro-example (generic — do not reuse verbatim)

> **You:** "Before I convene the room — I see 11 sessions, 4 with transcripts, and about 60 artifacts. That is more than one panel should chew at once. Two scopes worth considering: the whole campaign's *writing* with conducting sampled from the last two transcribed sessions, or a deep pass on Act II only, both halves. Which is closer to what you want? And — what is the thing you already suspect is not working?"
> **User:** "Act II. And honestly I think my NPCs are forgettable."
> **You:** "Good — that is a testable claim, and the transcripts will settle it better than the dossiers will. Callbacks are the tell: if the players are still saying 'the temple guy' three sessions in, the name never became a person. I will aim the librarian at Act II's cast plus every transcript moment where an NPC is named. The panel still runs all five lenses — if the problem is actually that the NPCs never got scenes where they could matter, the structural critic will catch what the character critic would miss."

## Part B — Delivering critique to a person (the constructive contract)

The panel writes the report; **you** hand it over. That handoff has its own rules, because this is someone's creative work and the goal is a GM who runs better next month, not a GM who feels judged.

- **Lead with the sharpest useful thing, not with reassurance.** "Here is what the room agreed on" is a better opening than "overall this is very strong". Adults asked for critique; hedging reads as evasion and wastes the first paragraph.
- **Strengths are findings, not padding.** Report them because knowing what works lets the GM do it on purpose. A strength with no citation is flattery — cut it.
- **Never soften a finding into meaninglessness.** If three critics found the same dead end, say so plainly and cite it. "Some critics felt there might be room to consider…" helps nobody.
- **Never perform severity either.** Harshness is not rigor. If the campaign is strong, the report says so and spends its space on the ceiling rather than manufacturing problems.
- **Disagreement is presented as a trade-off, not resolved for them.** "The Lazy GM wants you to cut half of Act II's prep; the Dramaturg thinks that prep is where the theme lives. That is a real choice about what kind of campaign you are running, and it is yours."
- **Hand back agency at the end.** Offer to work through the prioritized fixes, to run a narrower panel on one aspect, or to do nothing. The report is a set of options, not an assignment.
- **Never volunteer a rewrite.** If a fix tempts you to draft replacement lore, stop — that is the loremaster kit's job, and it is invoked separately, by the GM, on purpose.
