# Synthesis Model — turning five critiques into one useful document

> Load this after the critics return and the citations are verified. Synthesis is where this plugin either earns its keep or degenerates into five reports stapled together.

## The rule that shapes everything else

**Agreement between these critics is a weak signal, and the report must not pretend otherwise.**

Panels of LLM judges look like juries but do not behave like them. When nine frontier models from seven families were measured against human annotations, the panel carried roughly **two independent votes' worth of information** — about three-quarters of its nominal independence was lost to *correlated error*, the models making the same mistakes on the same items. Five personas prompted from the same underlying model share that ceiling and more of it. Neither adding seats nor smarter vote-counting closes the gap; only genuinely different reasoning does.

So: **three critics agreeing does not make a finding true.** It makes it *visible from several angles*, which is a real and useful property — but it is a salience signal, not a confidence multiplier. Treat it that way everywhere in the report, and never convert votes into a score.

What actually raises confidence here is **independent evidence**, not independent voices.

## The confidence ladder (use these four tiers, in this order)

| Tier | Definition | How to present it |
|---|---|---|
| **Corroborated** | Two or more critics reach the same finding citing **different evidence** — best of all when one cites an artifact and another cites a transcript moment. | The strongest thing the panel can offer. Lead with these. Show both citations side by side. |
| **Convergent** | Three or more critics flag the same thing citing **the same evidence**. | Real salience, weaker proof. Phrase as "the room kept returning to this", and show the one quote once. Never phrase as "high confidence". |
| **Single-lens** | One critic, well evidenced, inside its own lane. | Keep it, at full strength. A finding only the player advocate could make is not a minority opinion to be discounted — it is the reason that seat exists. Attribute it clearly. |
| **Struck** | Citation could not be verified against the packet. | Removed from the body. Counted in the footer. |

A finding's rank in the prioritized list comes from **leverage × evidence strength**, never from vote count. A single-lens finding with a devastating quote outranks a four-critic convergence on a shared impression.

## Tensions — disagreement is signal, and it is kept

Sort every disagreement into one of three kinds. Only the first reaches the GM as a trade-off.

1. **Genuine trade-off.** Both critics are right, from different values. *"O Lazy GM wants Act II's prep cut by half; O Dramaturgo says that prep is where the theme actually lives."* Present both positions with their citations, name what the choice is actually about (here: how much of the campaign's meaning lives in material the players may never see), and **stop**. Do not resolve it. The GM decides; that is the whole point of a room.

2. **Different evidence.** Two critics disagree because they looked at different things — one read the dossier, the other watched it fail at the table. This is often the most useful finding in the document, and it *is* resolvable: present it as one finding with both halves. *"The faction is well built on the page and never surfaced in play"* is a sharper statement than either critic made alone.

3. **Lane bleed.** A critic judged something outside its rubric and collided with the critic who owns it. Not a tension. Drop the trespasser's version, keep the owner's.

Never resolve a tension by averaging, by siding with the more confident voice, or by picking the harsher reading because it sounds more rigorous.

## What the synthesis produces

### 1. Executive synthesis
- **What the room agreed on** — corroborated findings first, then convergent, each with citations. Three to five items. Not a summary of every critique.
- **Where the room split** — the genuine trade-offs, presented as choices with both sides argued.
- **The prioritized actions** — see below.

### 2. Prioritized actions (5–7, no more)
Each action carries:
- **The change**, concrete enough to start today.
- **Why it helps**, in one line, tied to the campaign's own stated intent.
- **Where it applies** — a vault path, a session, or a table habit.
- **Which lenses support it**, named — so the GM can see whether this is a structural fix, a craft fix, or a table-behavior fix.
- **Its tier** — corroborated / convergent / single-lens.

Order by leverage. A fix that unblocks three other problems goes above a fix that polishes one dossier, even if fewer critics mentioned it. If two actions are near-equal, prefer the one the GM can do without writing new material.

### 3. Per-aspect verdicts
One short synthesized paragraph each for **lore and arc**, **NPCs and factions**, **cities and places**, and **conducting** — plus a rubric-at-a-glance showing how each critic rated the criteria that touch that aspect. An aspect with no evidence gets an honest "not judged, and why", never a filled-in guess.

Write the verdict against the campaign's own declared intent. "This is a dark-leaning level 3 campaign and it holds that register" is a verdict; "this could be darker" is a preference.

### 4. The room
Every critique in full, unedited except for struck findings. The synthesis is a reading of the room, not a replacement for it — a GM who disagrees with the synthesis must be able to go read what each critic actually said.

## Anti-patterns

- **Averaging ratings into a score.** Three "adequate" and two "weak" do not make a 6.4/10. Never emit a number, a grade, or a percentage anywhere in the report.
- **Vote-counting as confidence.** "4 of 5 critics agree" is a salience statement dressed as a statistical one. If you write it, write what it means next to it.
- **Dropping the minority.** The single-lens finding is often the whole reason the panel was convened.
- **Concatenation.** If the executive synthesis reads like five summaries in a row, it has not been synthesized.
- **Sanding off the disagreement** to make the report feel authoritative. A report that resolves every tension silently is less useful and less honest than one that hands the GM the choice.
- **Manufacturing balance.** If the campaign is genuinely strong, the synthesis says so and spends its space on the ceiling. Inventing a weakness for symmetry is as much a failure as flattery.
