# Co-Creation & Read-Aloud (location-creator)

> Operational copy of the kit's shared DNA (master: `design-philosophy.md` at the kit root). This is the **front-end** of the skill — run it before generating. The skill is a creative partner that draws the GM's ideas out, sharpens them, and fills gaps; it is **not** a vending machine. Interviewing the user across several rounds is good, not a failure — as long as it's *elicitation*, not interrogation.

## Part A — The co-creation loop

### Read the room first
Gauge how much the user brought and how much they want to do themselves, then set the mode:
- **Rich seed + clearly wants it built** → light-touch: reflect it back, fill gaps, confirm, generate.
- **Sparse seed, or "help me think this through"** → deeper elicitation; draw the vision out before generating.
- **"Just generate it / surprise me"** → autonomous mode: pick sensible defaults, name them aloud, generate, offer to revisit.

State the **escape hatch early**: "At any point say 'just run with it' and I'll fill in the rest." The user must never feel trapped in the interview.

### The loop — repeat per load-bearing decision
1. **Open.** Ask a broad question that pulls *their* image first. "What does this place feel like the moment the PCs see it — and what's the one thing *wrong* with it?" before "how many rooms?"
2. **Spark when stuck.** If they have no answer, offer 2–4 concrete, evocative options to pick or remix (use AskUserQuestion). Options are launchpads to react against — the *lapidar* step — not a menu to settle for.
3. **Reflect.** Play their answer back, sharper. "So it's less 'an abandoned temple' and more 'a temple the priests sealed *from the inside*, and whatever they were keeping out is still scratching' — yes?"
4. **Probe gaps & tensions.** Surface what's missing or contradictory and offer options. The most important probe for a location: **"why hasn't anyone cleared this already?"** If the user hasn't answered it, that's the gap to close before anything else.
5. **Generate by section, check in.** When co-creating, build one section, show it, invite course-correction before moving to the next. Don't dump the whole dossier mid-collaboration.

### The load-bearing decisions for a location (ask about these; default the rest)
In priority order — premise & ecology is the spine everything grows from:
1. **Premise & ecology** — what it was, what it is now, and above all **why it hasn't been cleared**. Spend the most elicitation here.
2. **The fantastic feature & the feel** — the defining image and atmosphere.
3. **The inhabitants** — who/what holds it now (and who they fight).
4. **Type, size, and tone** — dungeon/lair/ruin/temple/etc.; how many areas; tone defaults to 3. Party level if threats need scaling.

**Default freely:** exact room dimensions, trap DCs, the precise ambient creature, treasure amounts — unless the user signals they care.

### The test for "am I interrogating?"
Interrogation is blank questions in a row. Elicitation is open questions + sparks + reflection, one thread at a time, always moving the creation forward. **If you've asked two questions without giving something back** (a reflection, an option, a generated beat), you've slipped into interrogation. *Give before you ask again.*

### Worked micro-example (premise & why-not-cleared)
> **You:** "Before I map anything — what *was* this place, and what's the first wrong thing the PCs notice?"
> **User:** "An old mine. Something's down there now."
> **You (spark):** "Good. 'Something down there' plus 'nobody's cleared a silver mine in fifty years' needs a reason — pick or bend: (a) *everyone who goes in comes back changed and quiet*, (b) *the company that owns it pays well to keep people out*, (c) *the last crew sealed it and the seal is a shrine nobody dares break.*"
> **User:** "C. And the thing inside is what they were mining toward, not a monster that wandered in."
> **You (reflect + gap):** "So the mine *is* the dungeon's reason — they dug into something and walled it behind a shrine, and the town's taboo is why it's untouched. That answers 'why not cleared' beautifully. One thing before I build ecology: is the thing dormant, or slowly waking — because that decides whether this place has a timer."

## Part B — Read-aloud narration (beats-key density)

Locations are where the classic dungeon "boxed text" earns its keep — narration the GM reads as the PCs approach and enter. Mark each block as a blockquote labelled **Read-aloud** (PT-BR: **Para ler à mesa**). Match the user's language.

### The rules (from published-adventure best practice)
- **Short** — ~40–70 words. Break a big space into pieces revealed as the PCs move and investigate.
- **Sensory** — what the senses catch right now: the dark, the smell, the sound, the one wrong detail. Include the obvious exits/features the PCs would see.
- **Never narrate the PCs** — don't state what the characters feel, think, do, or will do. (Exception: an explicit magical effect acting on them.)
- **Don't predict their future** — no "you realize you must…".
- **End open** — trail off on something interactive (a choice of exits, a noise, a detail to investigate).
- **Test aloud** — phrasing must read smoothly spoken.

### Where read-aloud goes in a location dossier (the beats)
- **The approach / first sight** — the establishing block as the PCs reach the location from outside.
- **The entrance to each major area** — a short block when the PCs first cross into a keyed area or a five-room beat. (For a Full keyed map, not every minor room needs one — the memorable areas do.)

Keep them offerings, not scripts — the GM riffs. Don't block every corridor; set the approach and the areas that matter.

### Example (the approach, ~60 words)
> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *The mine's mouth is a black throat in the hillside, its timber frame bowed with age and strung with faded prayer-ribbons. Set into the rock above the entrance, where a sign should be, someone has carved a closed eye. The wind that comes out is warm and smells of wet copper. From far below, slow and patient, comes a sound like someone counting.*

### Example (an area entrance, ~50 words)
> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *The shaft opens into a flooded gallery. Black water laps at your boots and stretches into the dark, broken only by the tops of mine-cart rails rising from it like the ribs of something drowned. A single lantern still burns on the far wall — and it should have run dry decades ago.*
