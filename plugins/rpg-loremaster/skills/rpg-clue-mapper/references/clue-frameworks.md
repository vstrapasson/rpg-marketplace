# Clue Frameworks — the engine

The toolkit for mapping an investigation so it can't grind to a halt. Load for every clue map. Grounded in Justin Alexander's *The Alexandrian* — the **Three Clue Rule**, **node-based scenario design**, **revelation lists**, and **proactive nodes**.

> **About the examples here:** illustrative only — generic, or from public media (e.g. Critical Role's *Vox Machina*). **Never reuse them verbatim** — invent clues from the user's own mystery.

## 1. Revelations — the structural pass

Start from **conclusions, not clues.** A revelation is a thing you want the players to *figure out* ("the steward is the inside man"; "the shipments hide the contraband"; "the baroness ordered the killings"). List every revelation the mystery needs, then assign clues to each (Alexandrian's "structural pass"). For each revelation, record:
- **The truth** — stated in one plain line.
- **Core or optional** — *core* = the mystery can't resolve without it (the Three Clue Rule is mandatory here); *optional* = enriches understanding but isn't required for progress.
- **What it gates** — which act/beat it unlocks (often a campaign act turn).

A clue map is fundamentally a **list of revelations with clues hung off each** — build that spine first.

## 2. The Three Clue Rule

> For each conclusion you want the PCs to reach, include **at least three clues.**

The reasoning (Alexandrian): the players will **miss the first clue, ignore the second, and misinterpret the third** — so three is the floor for *core* revelations, not a luxury. One clue per conclusion is the single most common reason investigations collapse: a missed roll or a skipped room and the whole scenario stops.

**"Independent" is the key word.** Three clues that all sit in the same locked desk, or all require the same skill check, or all come from the same NPC, are effectively *one* clue — a single failure removes them all. Independence means: **different nodes, different discovery methods, different gates.** If a player kills the only witness, two more paths must survive.

## 3. Clue types — evidence vs. leads

Every clue is one of two things (label each):
- **Evidence** — points toward a **revelation** (the ledger that proves the steward is paid off; the wound that shows the weapon).
- **Lead** — points toward another **node** to investigate next (the address on the letter; the name the dying man gasps). Leads keep the investigation *moving*; evidence lets it *conclude*.

A healthy map has both: leads chain the nodes together; evidence resolves the revelations. A map of pure evidence with no leads leaves players with facts but nowhere to go; pure leads with no evidence is a chase that never concludes.

## 4. The node web & the inversion check

The world you've built is a set of **nodes** — locations, NPCs, factions, events, documents. Clues live *in* nodes and point *between* them. Map it:
- For each node, list the **clues inside it** and the **leads pointing to it**.
- Run the **inversion check** (Alexandrian's robust form of the rule): aim for **≥3 clues inside each key node** *and* **≥3 leads pointing to each key node.** When every node has three ways in and three things to find, the investigation flows no matter where the players start or what they skip.

This naturally satisfies the Three Clue Rule for the whole structure at once. Where a node can't support three of each, it shouldn't be load-bearing — move its critical clue somewhere better-connected.

## 5. Proactive backstops (the anti-stall net)

Even a robust map can stall (the table goes sideways, the players fixate). The fix is the **proactive node** (Alexandrian): instead of the PCs going to information, **the information comes to them.** For each *core* revelation, write one backstop — what happens if they miss every placed clue:
- A person **seeks them out** (an informant, a victim's relative, a frightened witness).
- A new **incident** drops the lead in their lap (another disappearance, an attack, a public accusation).
- The **antagonist's plan-in-motion** advances and exposes something (a shipment moves; a body surfaces).

The classic image: *"a guy with a gun walks through the door."* **Tie the backstop to the antagonist's plan** (from `rpg-campaign-foundation`) so the push feels diegetic — the world acting, not the GM rescuing. Backstops are the guarantee that the mystery progresses even when the dice and the players conspire against it.

## 6. Permissive finding & gating

- **Permissive clue-finding:** the map is a **floor, not a ceiling.** When players invent a clever, unplanned approach, reward it with real information. Three clues are the *guaranteed* paths; good play should open more.
- **Gating:** only **core revelations gate progress** (a core revelation is usually an act turn). **Never gate an act behind an optional clue** — optional clues deepen understanding and reward thoroughness, but locking progress behind them recreates the single-point-of-failure problem.
- **Core vs. bonus clues:** core clues must be *findable* (covered by the Three Clue Rule); bonus clues are extra texture (the esoteric detail that makes a clever player feel brilliant) — you can have one-of-a-kind bonus clues precisely because nothing depends on them.

## 7. Red herrings (opt-in only)

**Off by default.** The core map stays 100% robust — every core revelation has ≥3 true, independent clues. Red herrings are added **only when the user explicitly asks.**

When you do add them: the danger (Alexandrian) is that red herrings + the Three Clue Rule interact badly — players treat false leads with the same weight as real ones and over-invest, grinding the game. So **every red herring needs a recognition trigger**: a concrete, findable way for the players to realize it's a dead end (an alibi that checks out; a document that's clearly older than the crime). A false lead the players can *resolve and dismiss* adds paranoia and realism; one they can't is just a trap. Keep them few (one or two), and never let a red herring obscure a *core* clue.

## 8. Anti-patterns & the final check

**Anti-patterns:**
- **Single point of failure** — one clue → one revelation. The cardinal sin; redundancy fixes it.
- **The linear trail** — A→B→C with no branches; missing any link halts everything. Node-based design (multiple leads in) fixes it.
- **Clustered clues** — all three "independent" clues behind the same door/skill/NPC. Spread across node types.
- **Optional-gated progress** — an act locked behind a non-core discovery.
- **Rescue-by-fiat** — a backstop with no in-world cause. Tie it to the antagonist's plan.

**Final check (run before declaring done):** for **every core revelation**, confirm —
1. ≥3 clues, in **different nodes**, found by **different methods**?
2. No single failure (missed roll, dead NPC, skipped room) removes all of them?
3. A proactive backstop exists if they miss everything?
4. It gates only what it should, and nothing core is gated behind an optional clue?

If all four are yes for every core revelation, the mystery is grind-proof.
