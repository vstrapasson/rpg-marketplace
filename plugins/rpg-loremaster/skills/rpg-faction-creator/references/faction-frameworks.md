# Faction Frameworks — the design engine

The toolkit for building a faction as a *dynamic agent*. Load this for every faction. Grounded in Dungeon World Fronts (Impulse / Grim Portents / Impending Doom), Blades in the Dark (faction clocks), and Colville (factions as the engine of moral complexity).

## 1. The drive — the engine

Everything in a faction grows from what it wants and why. Three layers, from surface to root:

- **Goal** — concrete and nearly achievable, with a deadline pressure. *Test:* could a GM tell when it's accomplished? "Power" fails; "control every dock lease in the city before the winter charter renewal" passes.
- **Impulse** — the *why* beneath the goal; the emotional engine that makes them *act now* instead of waiting. Pick one dominant impulse:

  | Impulse | The faction is driven by… | Reads as |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Fear** | a threat they're trying to outrun or pre-empt | desperate, ruthless |
  | **Faith** | a belief that justifies anything | zealous, certain |
  | **Grievance** | a wrong they're avenging or correcting | bitter, righteous |
  | **Greed** | accumulation for its own sake | cold, expansive |
  | **Love** | protecting someone/something specific | sympathetic, dangerous |
  | **Duty** | an oath or role they cannot abandon | rigid, honorable-but-blind |
  | **Pride** | status, legacy, being proven right | vain, brittle |

- **Ideology / stance** — what they *believe* and **who benefits if they win** (the Colville tension). Always be able to state the case a sympathizer would make. A faction you can argue *for* generates real play; a faction that's just "the bad guys" generates a fight. Even a monstrous faction has an internal logic that feels like mercy or justice from inside.

**The one-line drive** (put it at the top of the dossier): *"[Faction] wants [goal] because [impulse], believing [ideology]."*

## 2. The plan in motion — the dynamic core (the signature of this skill)

This is what makes a faction an *agent* instead of a description. The faction is **already acting**, whether or not the PCs engage.

### Impending Doom
Where the plan ends if **the PCs never intervene**. The bell at the end. Rules of thumb (from Dungeon World):
- Sinister enough to grab attention, **not** world-ending — the PCs should feel they *could* ignore it (and shouldn't).
- Concrete and specific: "the Syndicate owns every dock and the harbor watch answers only to them" — not "the Syndicate becomes powerful."
- It's a *state of the world*, not a single act.

### Grim Portents — the steps to the doom
The ordered chain of concrete events from *now* to the doom. Each portent is a thing a GM can drop into a session as a sign the clock is ticking. They usually have a logical order (the bribe before the vote; the vote before the monopoly; the monopoly before the purge). Write **4–6 portents**, escalating.

- **Advance descriptively** when play makes it happen (the PCs saw the bribe land).
- **Advance prescriptively** as a GM move when the PCs stumble or ignore the faction (time passes; the next portent fires off-screen).
- Mark which portents are **visible** to the PCs (the rumor, the new toll) vs. **hidden** (the real purpose).

### Progress clock (Full only — optional GM tool)
Render the grim portents as a **4–6 segment clock**: each segment = one portent; filling the last = the impending doom. PF2e isn't a clock-based system, so present this explicitly as an *optional tracking tool*, not a rule. The clock is the thing a GM ticks **between sessions** to keep the faction moving in the background (Blades style: pick the faction, advance one or two segments, see what changes in the world).

**Compact** renders all of this as prose: *"Right now they're [doing X]. If nobody stops them, in a few months [the doom]. The signs along the way: [portent], then [portent], then [portent]."* **Full** adds the explicit clock.

## 3. The reaction ladder — how the faction pushes back

A faction is defined by how it *responds* to interference. Give five escalating rungs; one line each, **specific to this faction** (a cult courts differently than a noble house retaliates):

1. **Recruit / court** — the faction wants the PCs *in*, or useful. (An offer, a favor, a test.)
2. **Tolerate / watch** — the PCs are noticed but not yet a threat. (A tail, a quiet inquiry, a marked tab.)
3. **Pressure / warn** — a shot across the bow. (A threat to someone they love, a planted rumor, a withdrawn favor, a framed minor charge.)
4. **Retaliate** — direct, proportionate harm. (An ambush, a theft, a betrayal, a loved one taken.)
5. **Open war** — the faction commits to destroying the PCs. (Reserve this; it should feel earned.)

### Reaction table (Full only)
An explicit **"if the PCs do X → the faction does Y"** table for 3–5 likely PC moves (investigate them, rob them, expose them, join then betray, kill a lieutenant). This is the between-sessions tool that keeps the faction acting believably. Tie each response to the faction's *impulse* — a fear-driven faction over-reacts; a duty-driven faction responds by the book; a pride-driven faction can be goaded.

## 4. Faction archetypes (eight types)

Starting frames — pick one, then subvert at least one expectation. Each lists a typical goal-shape, structure, signature resource, native fracture, and dark-leaning angle.

| Type | Typical goal | Structure | Signature resource | Native fracture | Dark-leaning angle |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Guild** (trade/craft) | monopoly, charter, control of a market | masters → journeymen → apprentices | coin, legal standing, a chokepoint | old guard vs. reformers | protectionism that crushes outsiders; price-fixing as quiet violence |
| **Cult** (religious) | usher in / appease / become their god's vision | prophet → inner circle → faithful | fanatic labor, secret rites, a relic | true believers vs. cynics | the ideology that justifies anything; mercy that kills |
| **Noble house** | succession, land, legacy, a rival's ruin | head → heirs → retainers → clients | wealth, title, private soldiers, debt | siblings/heirs at odds | the gilded rot; disappearing dissenters; an old infernal bargain |
| **Criminal syndicate** | territory, rackets, the city's hidden half | boss → captains → crews | muscle, blackmail, smuggling routes | ambitious lieutenant | the only ones feeding the slums *and* the ones bleeding them |
| **Knightly order / inquisition** | defend a faith/realm, purge a threat | grandmaster → knights → squires | training, sanction, fortresses | doctrine vs. mercy | righteousness that became cruelty; the purge that overreaches |
| **Rebel cell / revolution** | overthrow, free, expose the powerful | cell leaders → operatives (compartmented) | popular sympathy, hidden caches | pragmatists vs. purists | becoming the thing they fight; the ends devouring the means |
| **Cabal / secret society** | hidden control, forbidden knowledge, a long plan | masked council → initiates by degree | secrets, infiltration, magic | a degree that wants more | the long game that treats people as pieces |
| **Mercenary company** | contracts, survival, a homeland of their own | captain → officers → companies | soldiers, reputation, a war-chest | loyalty to coin vs. cause | who they'll work for; the atrocity in the back-pay |

## 5. Internal-rot patterns (dark-leaning, tone 3)

At tone 3, a faction has **one** rotted part — not the whole. The contrast (clean ideal, rotted practice) is what makes it land. Patterns:

- **The ideal vs. the practice** — the order that protects the weak by disappearing the inconvenient; the charity that launders coin.
- **The compromised leader** — a noble cause run by someone who's made a dark bargain (a patron deity of the wrong kind, a debt to a fiend, a secret addiction to the power).
- **The faction-within** — a hardline wing operating beyond the leader's knowledge or control; a purge faction inside the reformers.
- **The founding lie** — the faction's origin story is false, and a few at the top know.
- **The cost nobody names** — membership requires something quietly monstrous (an oath sealed in blood, a name given to the cult, a year of your life).

Keep the rest of the faction *sincere* so the rot is a discovery, not a label. A paladin should be able to *almost* join before learning the truth.

## 6. Orthogonality & the sympathizer test

A faction exists *relative to* others and to the PCs. Two checks before finalizing:

- **Orthogonality** (consistent with the region/city kit): this faction's conflict with each neighbor should differ. If it opposes everyone for the same reason, it's a generic antagonist. Define *who else wants what they want* (rivals) and *who they'd ally with against a third party*.
- **The sympathizer test:** write one sentence making the faction's case as a believer would. If you can't — if the only case is "they're evil" — the faction is cardboard. Add a true grievance, a real good they protect, or a sympathetic wing. (Even Asmodean tyrants argue order against chaos.)

## Quick build checklist
1. One-line drive stated (goal + impulse + ideology)?
2. Sympathizer test passed (you can argue *for* them)?
3. Impending doom is concrete and not world-ending?
4. 4–6 grim portents, ordered, some visible / some hidden?
5. Leader has a personal want *distinct* from the faction's, and a voice line?
6. At least one lieutenant is a lever or a fracture?
7. Cost of membership named?
8. Exactly one rotted part at tone 3 (rest sincere)?
9. Reaction ladder is specific to *this* faction's impulse?
10. Every hook anchors to drive / lieutenant / secret / doom, with ≥1–2 moral-cost at tone 3?
11. Off-stage handoffs named (leader → npc, seat → location)?
