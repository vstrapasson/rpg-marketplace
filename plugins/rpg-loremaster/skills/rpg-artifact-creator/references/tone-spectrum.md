# Tone Spectrum — the 1–5 Dark-Leaning Scale (artifact edition)

Defines the five tone levels this skill can generate. Default is **level 3 (dark-leaning)**. Read this whenever generating at a level other than 3, or whenever unsure how much shadow to put into an artifact. (Mirrors the kit's shared scale, reframed for artifact elements — the price, the will, the provenance, and how much the gift costs.) **System-agnostic** — this is a tonal dial, not a mechanical one.

## The scale

| Level | Label | Reference artifacts | What it feels like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Luminous classical | the holy avenger, the wishing ring, the hero's destined blade | Clean boons; the gift is pure, the bearer worthy, the cost token or none. |
| **2** | Heroic adventure | most published wondrous items, a blessed relic with a small catch | A real gift with a mild quirk or upkeep; nothing the bearer regrets. |
| **3 ⭐ default** | **Dark-leaning** | the One Ring early on, Elric's Stormbringer at a slow burn, a saint's relic that asks for blood | A genuinely good gift with a rotted edge — a price the bearer pays in stages and *chooses* to keep paying. The will and the cost pull against the boon. |
| **4** | Dark fantasy | Stormbringer in full, a crown that rules but hollows its wearer | Mostly cost; the gift is real but the corruption runs the bearer's life. |
| **5** | Grimdark | a soul-devouring artifact that was always a trap | A predator wearing a treasure; the gift is the lure, the bearer is the meal. |

## How each level changes the artifact

### Level 1 (Luminous classical)
- The gift is clean; the price is symbolic (worthiness, a vow) or absent.
- The will, if any, is benevolent and aligned with the bearer.
- Provenance is noble — forged by a god, a hero, a good order.

### Level 2 (Heroic adventure)
- One mild catch: an upkeep, a restriction, a quirk the bearer works around.
- The will is faint; the artifact mostly serves.
- A small shadow in the provenance, easily borne.

### Level 3 ⭐ (Dark-leaning — DEFAULT)
- **Mix 5:3:2** — the gift is mostly a boon, with real ambiguity and **one** genuinely troubling cost.
- **The temptation test is mandatory:** the gift must be good enough that a reasonable bearer *wants* to keep using it despite the price. If no one would be tempted, you wrote a 1–2 (clean boon) or a 4–5 (obvious trap).
- **The price comes in stages with a tell**, and the bearer chooses each step deeper — that choice is the drama.
- **The provenance carries the shadow** — the price paid in the *making* is specific and active in what the artifact wants now.
- **The will pulls** — the artifact nudges the bearer toward a deed, a place, a person, and the price escalates toward it.
- At least 1–2 hooks carry a **moral cost** — claiming it helps the wrong people, or unmaking it costs someone who needs it.
- A decent bearer should be able to *almost* justify keeping it before the cost truly surfaces.

### Level 4 (Dark fantasy)
- Mix flips to 2:3:5 — mostly cost, with a few real boons.
- The corruption tips toward control: the will starts overriding the bearer.
- Each use feeds the will visibly; the bearer is losing the bargain and can tell.
- Sympathy for the bearer survives, but keeping the artifact is clearly a mistake.

### Level 5 (Grimdark)
- Mix 0:2:8 — no clean gift remains.
- The boon was always bait; the artifact was made to consume its bearer.
- The "price" is the artifact's whole purpose; there is no winning bargain.
- Add a content advisory in the off-stage notes.

## Applying level 3 specifically (the default checklist)

1. **Temptation test:** is the gift good enough that a reasonable person keeps using it despite the cost? If not, raise the gift or lower the price.
2. **One genuinely troubling cost**, staged — name it; keep the gift mostly a boon for contrast.
3. **The price escalates toward the will:** each stage is the artifact getting more of its way.
4. **The provenance's price is active** — what it cost to make still shapes what it wants (cause → effect), not just a grim origin story.
5. **A tell at each stage** so the players can read the danger and choose anyway.
6. **≥1–2 moral-cost hooks** — mark them.
7. **The cost makes problems** — for the bearer and, ideally, for the party.

The goal of level 3 is **contrast**: a gift good enough to keep, and a price steep enough to dread. Pure boon = a vending-machine reward. Pure trap = a monster to disarm. Dark-leaning = a bargain the bearer makes with their eyes open and regrets by degrees.

## Common mistakes

- **Over-darkening.** If the user asked for 3 and you wrote a thing no reasonable person would carry, you wrote a 4–5. Pull back: restore the genuinely good gift and the bargain worth making.
- **Under-darkening.** If the artifact has a clean gift, no staged price, and no moral-cost hook, it's a 1–2. Add the cost, the tell, and make one hook hurt.
- **Decorative darkness (the "grim origin = depth" trap).** Don't bolt on a bloody backstory for flavor. The price paid in the making must *connect to what the artifact wants and costs now* — otherwise it's set dressing.

## Sliding between levels
If the user wants a level-3 artifact but "darker the longer you hold it," generate at base 3 and let the *later stages* of the price climb toward 4 while keeping the early gift and tells at 3. Note the deviation in off-stage so downstream skills and the reviewer stay consistent.
