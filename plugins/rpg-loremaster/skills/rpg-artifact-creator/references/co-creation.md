# Co-Creation & Read-Aloud (artifact-creator)

> Operational copy of the kit's shared DNA (master: `design-philosophy.md` at the kit root). This is the **front-end** of the skill — run it before generating. The skill is a creative partner that draws the GM's ideas out, sharpens them, and fills gaps; it is **not** a vending machine. Interviewing the user across several rounds is good, not a failure — as long as it's *elicitation*, not interrogation.

## Part A — The co-creation loop

### Read the room first
Gauge how much the user brought and how much they want to do themselves, then set the mode:
- **Rich seed + clearly wants it built** → light-touch: reflect it back, fill gaps, confirm, generate.
- **Sparse seed, or "help me think this through"** → deeper elicitation; draw the vision out before generating.
- **"Just generate it / surprise me"** → autonomous mode: pick sensible defaults, name them aloud, generate, offer to revisit.

State the **escape hatch early**: "At any point say 'just run with it' and I'll fill in the rest." The user must never feel trapped in the interview.

### The loop — repeat per load-bearing decision
1. **Open.** Ask a broad question that pulls *their* image first. "Where did this thing come from — who made it, and what did making it cost?" before "what does it look like?"
2. **Spark when stuck.** If they have no answer, offer 2–4 concrete, evocative options to pick or remix (use AskUserQuestion). Options are launchpads to react against — the *lapidar* step — not a menu to settle for.
3. **Reflect.** Play their answer back, sharper. "So it's less 'a holy sword' and more 'a saint's femur the church reforged into a blade, and it still wants to be buried' — yes?"
4. **Probe gaps & tensions.** Surface what's missing or contradictory and offer options. "You want it to *protect* the bearer *and* corrupt them — is the protection the bait, and the corruption the price it charges for it?"
5. **Generate by section, check in.** When co-creating, build one section, show it, invite course-correction before moving to the next. Don't dump the whole dossier mid-collaboration.

### The load-bearing decisions for an artifact (ask about these; default the rest)
In priority order — the **node** is the engine everything grows from, so anchor the vision there first:
1. **Provenance + the will.** Where it came from (maker, original purpose, the price paid to make it) and what it *wants* now. Spend the most elicitation here; the gift, the price, and the hooks all follow from it.
2. **The gift and the price.** What it does (the bait — genuinely good), and what it costs the bearer (staged, with a tell, escalating toward the will).
3. **Desire-lines + what it unlocks.** Who is already reaching for it and why, and what its arrival changes in the story.
4. **Look, the exit, and tone.** Its sensory tell; how it can leave play; tone defaults to 3.

**Default freely:** the exact name, fine appearance detail, secondary desire-lines, the precise exit rite — unless the user signals they care.

### The test for "am I interrogating?"
Interrogation is blank questions in a row. Elicitation is open questions + sparks + reflection, one thread at a time, always moving the creation forward. **If you've asked two questions without giving something back** (a reflection, an option, a generated beat), you've slipped into interrogation. *Give before you ask again.*

### Worked micro-example (the node)
> **You:** "Before a name or a shape — where did this thing come from, and what did making it cost?"
> **User:** "It's a bell. The drowned town under the bay rings it."
> **You (spark):** "Eerie — and a perfect seed. Three ways its making could carry a price — pick or bend: (a) it was cast from the melted-down votive coins of everyone the flood took, so it *remembers* them; (b) a bellfounder drowned her own apprentice in the mold to give it a voice that carries underwater; (c) it was never made — it *grew* on the seabed where the church sank, and it tolls on its own."
> **User:** "B — and the voice can call the drowned up to walk."
> **You (reflect + gap):** "So the gift is command over the drowned dead, the price is paid in the bellfounder's coin — a life owed for a life called — and the will is the apprentice still in the bronze, wanting *out*. One thing before I draft: who wants this bell badly enough to kill for it, and is it the town that lost their dead, or something that wants them kept down?"

## Part B — Read-aloud narration (presence & dread)

An artifact's read-aloud is about **presence and dread** — how it feels in the room, how it stirs, what the price looks like taking hold — not a tour of its history. Mark each block as a blockquote labelled **Read-aloud** (PT-BR: **Para ler à mesa**). Match the user's language.

### The rules (from published-adventure best practice)
- **Short** — ~40–70 words.
- **Sensory** — what the senses catch right now: the weight in the hand, the cold off the metal, the sound it makes that nothing else makes.
- **Never narrate the PCs** — don't state what the characters feel, think, do, or will do. (Exception: an explicit magical compulsion the artifact imposes.)
- **Don't predict their future** — no "you'll come to depend on it".
- **End open** — trail off on something interactive or unresolved.
- **Test aloud** — phrasing must read smoothly spoken; kill tongue-twisters and accidental alliteration.

### Where read-aloud goes for an artifact (the beats)
- **Entrance** — how it reaches the PCs' hands: the dramatic beat of acquisition (handed from a corpse, dug from a reliquary, won, inherited, stolen).
- **The tell** — the sensory cue when the artifact stirs and the price climbs a stage (the thing that warns the players it is waking).
- **One signature moment** — a small scene of the price taking hold: the will getting its way, the cost made visible.

Keep them offerings, not scripts — the GM will riff. Don't read-aloud the provenance; convey presence and dread.

### Example (an entrance, ~60 words)
> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *The diver's body comes up roped at the ankles, and the bell comes up with it — cradled against the chest like something nursed. It is the green-black of old bronze, no bigger than a helm, and when the deck rolls it does not swing. The clapper hangs dead still. The water running off it is warm.*

### Example (a tell, ~50 words)
> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *Somewhere under the floor, a single low note sounds — though no one has touched the bell. It comes again, patient, the way a thing knocks when it knows you are home. The lamp-flames lean toward the sound. The salt on the windows beads and runs upward.*
