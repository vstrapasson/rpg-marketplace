# Artifact Frameworks — the design engine

The toolkit for building an artifact as a *narrative node*, not a powerful object. Load this for every artifact. **System-agnostic** — about provenance, will, and cost, not item levels or runes. Grounded in TTRPG treasure-design craft: The Alexandrian on "treasure with context" and node-based design, Sly Flourish's "lazy" item recipe (history + one evocative effect), Dungeon World's fiction-first magic (the *tell*, the custom move), and the dark-fantasy principle that magic always demands a price.

## 1. The narrative-node model — the engine

The difference between a forgettable item and one the table fights wars over is that the memorable one **does something to the story.** The Alexandrian's principle is the bedrock: *"the place where you picked up your +2 sword is only the tiniest fraction of the tales you'll forge with it"* — so design the artifact by its **potential to move the plot**, not by the moment of discovery. Treasure commands the players' undivided attention; spend that attention on meaning, not on numbers.

So an artifact is a **node** in the campaign — a thing with edges running out to people, places, and the central truth. Every artifact must answer four questions:

1. **Where did it come from?** — its **provenance**: who made it, why, and (for dark-leaning) *what price was paid* in the making. An artifact with no maker and no origin is loot.
2. **What does it want now?** — its **will**: the unfinished purpose it carries, the thing it pulls its bearer toward (§2).
3. **Who wants it, and why?** — its **desire-lines**: the factions, NPCs, and powers already reaching for it. These are the node's edges into the rest of the campaign.
4. **What changes when it enters play?** — what its arrival *unlocks or breaks* in the story.

**The one-line concept** (put it at the top of the dossier): *"[Name] — [what it is] that [its will], and [who] will [do what] to hold it."*

**The test:** if the dossier describes a powerful object that nobody is moving for and that changes nothing when it appears, you wrote treasure, not a node. Fix it by leading with what it *wants* and who is *acting* to get it.

## 2. The "what does it want?" probe — the will

Across the craft, *intent* is the line between a relic and loot. Find the will with a three-part probe:

- **Maker** — who forged it (a dying god, a grieving artificer, a cult that fed it something)?
- **Original purpose** — what it was *made to do*.
- **Current unfulfilled goal** — what it has *not yet finished*, or has been twisted toward since.

That unfulfilled goal is the **pull** the artifact exerts on whoever carries it — the nudge toward a deed, a place, a person. The will is what makes the artifact feel like an agent rather than a tool, and it is what the **price** (§4) escalates toward: the cost is the item slowly *getting its way.*

## 3. The function / story / cost triad

Every artifact answers three questions. Skip one and you know the tier you've written:

- **Function** — what it *does* (the gift). Pure function = a stat block.
- **Story** — what it *means* in the world (provenance + will + desire-lines). Function + story = memorable.
- **Cost** — what it *demands* of the bearer (the price). Function + story + cost = **the engine of a dark campaign.**

Render the **gift evocatively, never as a "+X."** Sly Flourish's recipe is a useful floor: a sentence of campaign-specific history + one strange, evocative effect beats a numeric bonus every time. The gift is the **bait** — it must be genuinely good, or the cost tempts no one (§4).

## 4. Designing the price (corruption stages + tell)

This is the dark-leaning core, and the place most cursed items fail. The genre's spine is *magic demands a price* — but a price only works if it is a **temptation**, not a tax.

- **The gift must be genuinely good.** Corruption only bites if the bearer *wants* to keep using the thing despite the cost. A debuff attached to a weak item is just bad loot.
- **The price comes in stages.** Don't apply the full cost at once. Lay out 3–5 **stages**, each deeper than the last, each triggered by *using* the gift or by feeding the will. The bearer **chooses** each step deeper — that choice is the drama.
- **Each stage has a tell.** Borrow Dungeon World's *tell*: a sensory cue that the item is stirring / the price is climbing (the veins darkening, the whispering getting words, the reflection lagging a half-second). The tell lets the *players* read the danger and decide anyway.
- **The price escalates toward the will.** Tie the corruption to what the artifact *wants* (§2): each stage is the item getting more of its way. That makes the cost feel authored, not arbitrary.

**Two dark-tone anti-patterns to avoid:**
- **The curse that's just a debuff.** A flat penalty with no temptation and no narrative pull. Fix: make the gift good, give the cost stages and a tell, tie it to the will.
- **Punishment without agency.** A curse the bearer can't see coming, can't engage with, and can't choose around. Fix: tells + stages + an in-fiction way to resist, slow, or feed it.

## 5. Tier note — why artifacts/relics differ (the exit matters)

This skill builds the **top tier** — unique, legendary, theme-bearing objects — not mundane gear or minor magic items (those are out of scope; the party buys them). The design question that's distinct at this tier: **how does it leave play?**

- At lower tiers an item just gets sold, swapped, or out-leveled. An **artifact's exit is part of its design** — often it *can't* be casually destroyed, and getting rid of it is a quest in itself (a specific rite, a specific place, a specific cost). The exit can be as memorable as the entrance, and at this tier you owe the table both.
- The will (§2) and the price (§4) usually make the exit *expensive*: the artifact resists being unmade, because being unmade thwarts what it wants.

## 6. Common failure modes

- **The magic Walmart.** Treating artifacts like shop stock — interchangeable, purchasable, meaningless. Fix: an artifact is rare, granted by the story, and connected to the campaign by its desire-lines.
- **The +X treadmill / the item that's just a number.** A bonus the bearer swaps out next level. A flat modifier is *mundane*; if it's truly magical it should do something *strange*. Fix: render the gift as fiction, not arithmetic.
- **The artifact with no cost.** A world-shaking object with no price is a stat block in a crown. Fix: §4 — gift + staged price.
- **The artifact with no exit.** A campaign-defining item that can be tossed in a river. Fix: §5 — give it a real way to leave play.
- **The static treasure.** Power, history, appearance — but nobody wants it and nothing changes when it appears. Fix: §1 — lead with the will and the desire-lines.

## 7. Coherence — the node test and the price test

An artifact coheres when **every element serves the node or the price.** Pass each element through two checks:

- **The node test:** does this element connect the object to the campaign — its provenance, its will, a desire-line, or what it unlocks? An element that ties to nothing is decoration. Cut it or connect it.
- **The price test:** does this element deepen what the bearer pays — a stage, a tell, a temptation, the will getting its way? A gift with no answering cost fails this test.

An element that serves *neither* the node nor the price does not belong in the dossier.

## 8. (Optional) PF2e bridge — one line

If the table plays Pathfinder 2e, map the **gift** onto a relic (relic seed → aspects → gifts that grow with the bearer) or an artifact (which carries a destruction clause — your §5 exit) — but keep this skill's output system-agnostic and do **not** add stat blocks, item levels, or Archives of Nethys links here.

## Quick build checklist
1. One-line concept stated (name + what it is + will + who wants it)?
2. **Provenance** named — maker, original purpose, and the price paid to make it?
3. **Will** found via the probe (maker + purpose + unfinished goal → the pull)?
4. At least 1–2 **desire-lines** — named actors already reaching for it?
5. What it **unlocks/breaks** on entering play, stated?
6. **Gift** rendered evocatively (not a "+X"), and genuinely good (the bait)?
7. **Price** in 3–5 stages, each with a **tell**, escalating toward the will?
8. An **exit** — how it leaves play (possibly a quest)?
9. Node test and price test passed (no element serving neither)?
10. 4–6 hooks growing from the node (≥1–2 moral-cost at tone 3)?
11. Off-stage handoffs named (maker/guardian/hunter → npc; who hunts it → faction; where it's kept → a location-creator; clues → clue-mapper)?
