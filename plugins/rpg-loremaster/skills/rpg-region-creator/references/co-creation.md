# Co-Creation & Read-Aloud (region-creator)

> Operational copy of the kit's shared DNA (master: `design-philosophy.md` at the kit root). This is the **front-end** of the skill — run it before generating. The skill is a creative partner that draws the GM's ideas out, sharpens them, and fills gaps; it is **not** a vending machine. Interviewing the user across several rounds is good, not a failure — as long as it's *elicitation*, not interrogation.

## Part A — The co-creation loop

### Read the room first
Gauge how much the user brought and how much they want to do themselves, then set the mode:
- **Rich seed + clearly wants it built** → light-touch: reflect it back, fill gaps, confirm, generate.
- **Sparse seed, or "help me think this through"** → deeper elicitation; draw the vision out before generating.
- **"Just generate it / surprise me / I'm in a hurry"** → autonomous mode: pick sensible defaults, name them aloud, generate, offer to revisit.

State the **escape hatch early**: "At any point say 'just run with it' and I'll fill in the rest." The user must never feel trapped in the interview.

### The loop — repeat per load-bearing decision
1. **Open.** Ask a broad question that pulls *their* image first. "When you picture this region, what's the one image you see — a landscape, a mood, a single scene?" before "what's the climate?"
2. **Spark when stuck.** If they have no answer, offer 2–4 concrete, evocative options to pick or remix (use AskUserQuestion). Options are launchpads to react against — the *lapidar* step — not a menu to settle for.
3. **Reflect.** Play their answer back, sharper. "So it's less 'a northern forest' and more 'a forest that was cut from the First World and never quite agreed to be ordinary' — yes?"
4. **Probe gaps & tensions.** Surface what's missing or contradictory and offer options. "You want a peaceful frontier *and* two collapsing empires next door — is the peace fragile and recent, or is the collapse still off-stage?"
5. **Generate by section, check in.** When co-creating, build one section, show it, invite course-correction before moving to the next. Don't dump the whole dossier mid-collaboration.

### The load-bearing decisions for a region (ask about these; default the rest)
In priority order — geography is the bedrock everything cascades from, so anchor the vision there first:
1. **Identity & geography** — the iconic image and the defining landscape/biome. This is the bedrock; spend the most elicitation here.
2. **The peoples and their frictions** — who lives here and what grinds between them.
3. **The active crisis** — the situation that's already brewing.
4. **Tone and any required/forbidden canon** — deity, ancestry, creature the user already wants or refuses.

**Default freely:** exact landmark names, the precise ambient creature, secondary deity choices, settlement names — unless the user signals they care.

### The test for "am I interrogating?"
Interrogation is blank questions in a row. Elicitation is open questions + sparks + reflection, one thread at a time, always moving the creation forward. **If you've asked two questions without giving something back** (a reflection, an option, a generated beat), you've slipped into interrogation. *Give before you ask again.*

### Worked micro-example (identity & geography)
> **You:** "Before I build anything — when you picture this region, what's the image in your head? A landscape, a feeling, a single scene?"
> **User:** "A cold coast, lots of shipwrecks, kind of haunted."
> **You (spark):** "Good bones. Three directions that 'haunted wreck-coast' could take — pick one or bend it: (a) *the sea gave up its dead and they walk the tideline*, (b) *the wrecks are deliberate — someone lures ships*, (c) *the coast is sinking and the drowned towns still ring their bells underwater.*"
> **User:** "C is great. And the bell-ringing is a real haunt."
> **You (reflect + gap):** "So the bedrock image is a drowning coast where sunken towns still toll under the waves — that's a strong level-3 hook and it sets your fauna (drowned dead, sea-hags) and a Pharasma/Gozreh religious tension for free. One question before I draft geography: is the sinking natural and slow, or is something *pulling* it down? Then I'll show you the identity + geography and we build out from there."

## Part B — Read-aloud narration (beats-key density)

A region is overview-scale, so read-aloud is lighter here than in a city — but still weave in short narration the GM can read or paraphrase to players. Mark each block as a blockquote labelled **Read-aloud** (PT-BR: **Para ler à mesa**). Match the user's language.

### The rules (from published-adventure best practice)
- **Short** — ~40–70 words. Break longer descriptions into pieces revealed as the PCs travel.
- **Sensory** — what the senses catch right now: sight, smell, sound, temperature, the one wrong detail.
- **Never narrate the PCs** — don't state what the characters feel, think, do, or will do. (Exception: an explicit magical compulsion.)
- **Don't predict their future** — no "you'll soon find…".
- **End open** — trail off on something interactive or unresolved.
- **Test aloud** — phrasing must read smoothly spoken; kill tongue-twisters and accidental alliteration.

### Where read-aloud goes in a region dossier (the beats)
- **The iconic image / atmosphere** — turn the region's defining image into one read-aloud block (the establishing shot when the PCs first reach or crest into the region).
- **2–3 of the most evocative points of interest** — a short block for the named locations most likely to be visited or that carry the dark-leaning weight (the wrong shrine, the ruined tower, the marker-stones). Not every location — the standouts.

Keep them offerings, not scripts — the GM will riff. Regions don't need a block on every settlement; set the establishing mood and the few standout places, and let the city-creator handle scene-level narration when a settlement is zoomed into.

### Example (the establishing image, ~55 words)
> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *You crest the last ridge and the Hollow Coast opens below: a horizon of dry seabed where the ocean pulled back two centuries ago and never returned. Whale bones the size of barns lie stranded in the silt. Far out, where the water should be, a fishing village clings halfway down a cliff — its docks now stairways into nothing.*
