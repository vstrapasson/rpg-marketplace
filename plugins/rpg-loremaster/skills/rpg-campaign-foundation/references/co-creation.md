# Co-Creation & Read-Aloud (campaign-foundation)

> Operational copy of the kit's shared DNA (master: `design-philosophy.md` at the kit root). This is the **front-end** of the skill — run it before generating. For *this* skill it matters more than for any other: a campaign foundation is the GM's most personal creative act, so the skill is a **thinking partner that draws the vision out**, never a vending machine. Expect several rounds of interview. That's the point — as long as it's *elicitation*, not interrogation.

> **About the examples in this file:** they are illustrative only — generic, or drawn from published media (e.g. Critical Role's *Vox Machina*). **Never reuse them verbatim in output.** Invent fresh material from the user's own campaign. (Examples are teaching aids, not templates — reusing them would bleed unrelated flavor into the user's campaign.)

## Part A — The co-creation loop

### Read the room first
- **Vivid seed (a villain, a strong image, a theme already in mind)** → reflect it back sharper, fill gaps, draft the spine.
- **Vague seed ("something dark with a corrupt church", "a heist campaign")** → draw the engine out before drafting; this is where the skill earns its keep.
- **"Just pitch me something / surprise me"** → generate a spine with sensible defaults, name them aloud, then refine together.

State the **escape hatch early**: "Say 'just run with it' anytime and I'll draft the whole spine and we tune from there."

### The loop — repeat per load-bearing decision
1. **Open.** Pull *their* image first. "Who or what is the threat — and what does it actually *want*, underneath?" before "what level do they start at?"
2. **Spark when stuck.** Offer 2–4 concrete, evocative options to pick or remix (use AskUserQuestion). Launchpads to react against — the *lapidar* step — not a menu to settle for.
3. **Reflect.** Play it back sharper, turning a generic idea into a specific one with a hidden engine.
4. **Probe gaps & tensions.** Surface what's missing or contradictory. The most generative probe in this whole skill: **"what's really going on that the players don't know yet?"** If that's unanswered, close it before anything else.
5. **Draft by section, check in.** Draft the truth, show it, refine; then pillars; then acts. Don't dump a whole foundation mid-collaboration — co-build it.

### The load-bearing decisions for a campaign (ask about these; default the rest)
In priority order — the central truth is the engine everything grows from:
1. **The central truth + the antagonist's true motivation** — the secret engine. Spend the most elicitation here; everything else cascades from it.
2. **The thematic pillars** — what the campaign is *about* emotionally (often these surface naturally once the truth is sharp).
3. **The shape** — antagonist present/sealed/returning, the visible agent, rough act structure.
4. **Scope, tone, and ready-made vs. original** — one arc or full campaign; tone defaults to 3; PF2e canon adopted or original world.

**Default freely:** exact NPC names, macguffin counts, precise level breaks, secondary signs — unless the user signals they care.

### The test for "am I interrogating?"
Interrogation is blank questions in a row. Elicitation is open questions + sparks + reflection, one thread at a time, always moving the creation forward. **If you've asked two questions without giving something back** (a reflection, an option, a drafted beat), you've slipped into interrogation. *Give before you ask again.* For a brainstorm skill especially, the user should feel they're discovering their own campaign, not filling a form.

### Worked micro-example (finding the central truth — the heart of this skill)
*(Illustrative only — a generic seed, invented on the spot. Do not reuse.)*
> **You:** "Before anything else — who or what is the threat, and what's really going on that the players won't know at the start?"
> **User:** "A frontier mining town that's weirdly prosperous. I want something's wrong under it."
> **You (spark):** "Good engine — 'too prosperous' begs the question *at whose expense?* Pick or bend: (a) *the town feeds its 'departed' to whatever's in the deep vein, and the council calls it emigration*, (b) *the prosperity is a bargain someone signed generations ago, and the bill is finally due*, (c) *the ore itself is changing the people who handle it, and no one will say so.*"
> **User:** "(b) — a bargain coming due."
> **You (reflect + probe):** "So the secret engine is: the town's good fortune is a debt, signed long ago, now being collected — and the people in charge know and are hiding it. That's your whole spine; every sign and act turn can be the debt surfacing. One question that sets the tone of the entire campaign: *what was promised, and to whom?* A devil's contract plays very differently from a pact with something in the hills. Want to brainstorm it, or should I offer three?"

## Part B — Read-aloud (light in this skill)

A campaign foundation is **GM-facing** — it's planning, not table narration. So read-aloud is minimal here. The **one** natural beat:

- **The opening hook** — the session-one situation the GM can read to the players to start the campaign. One block, ~40–70 words, sensory, **never narrating the PCs**, ending open (a hook to act on).

Everything else in the document is GM-facing prose. Don't manufacture read-aloud for the truth, the pillars, or the planning — those are for the GM's eyes. (Table-facing narration is the job of the downstream skills — region/city/location — when the campaign zooms in.)

### Example (an opening hook, ~50 words — generic, illustrative)
> **Read-aloud / Para ler à mesa:**
> *The road crests the ridge at dusk and the town spreads in the valley below — every chimney smoking, every window warm, the wheel of the ore-mill turning steady against the dark. It looks like the most welcoming place you've seen in weeks. Not one person is out in the streets.*
