# Campaign Frameworks — the design engine

The toolkit for building a campaign foundation. Load for every campaign. Grounded in Dungeon World Fronts (the antagonist's plan + impending doom), The Alexandrian ("don't prep plots, prep situations" + revelation lists), and Sly Flourish (campaign hook, campaign truths, the one-pager, spiral development). The structure mirrors a real, validated GM planning document this skill was modeled on.

> **About the examples in this file:** illustrative only — generic, or from published media (e.g. Critical Role's *Vox Machina*: the Briarwoods' takeover of Whitestone, the Chroma Conclave of dragons, a hidden god of secrets). **Never reuse them verbatim** — invent fresh material from the user's own campaign. Examples teach the shape; they are not templates, and copying them would bleed unrelated flavor into the user's game.

## 1. The central truth — the engine

A campaign's bedrock is **what's really going on that the players don't know yet** — the hidden situation that every sign, act, and clue serves. Build it in three moves:

- **What's really going on** — the buried situation. Often: a powerful entity's true nature, a historical lie, a coming event, a secret the world keeps.
- **The antagonist's true motivation** — *why*, beneath the surface. The strongest dark-leaning antagonists are **arguable from the inside** — you can state their case as a sympathizer would. Apply the **"is the villain actually right?" test** and, at tone 3, leave it *open* (don't confirm or deny to the players). "Wants power" is not a motivation; *"a general who walls in a plague-struck quarter to save the city — sacrificing hundreds to spare thousands, and believing she is merciful"* is.
- **The cost of the truth** — why it's buried, who buried it, and what it costs to know. (Example: the heroes who "saved" the realm a generation ago actually struck a bargain that's now coming due, and the temple has spent that generation hiding it — knowing the truth makes you its next target.)

**State the truth in one or two sentences at the top of the GM section.** If you can't, the campaign has no engine yet — keep working. Everything downstream (theme, acts, signs, clues) is the truth surfacing in stages.

> The truth is GM-facing and revealed gradually — or never completely. That gradual revelation *is* the campaign's spine.

## 2. The antagonist & the plan in motion

Borrow `rpg-faction-creator`'s Fronts logic at campaign scale:

- **The antagonist** — who/what embodies the truth. Decide their **presence**: *present* (active now), *sealed/dormant* (the campaign is about their return), *returning* (mid-revival), or *working through agents* (hidden behind a hand). Hidden or returning antagonists are especially strong for dark-leaning — the threat is the *approach*, and the dread precedes the reveal. (In *Vox Machina*, the Briarwoods rule openly while the true power behind them — a god of secrets seeking to ascend — stays hidden for a long arc.)
- **The plan in motion** — what's advancing **right now, independent of the PCs**. The plan has **steps** (grim portents) and an **impending doom** — where it ends if the PCs never intervene. Concrete, not "the world ends": *"the cult completes the binding and the city's only aquifer answers to them"* or *"four dragons divide the realm between them and rule the ashes"* (the Chroma Conclave) beats "darkness covers the land."
- **The visible agent / mid-boss** — the hand the PCs meet *first*, since the true antagonist is often hidden or absent early (the smuggler who hired them answers to a captain they never see; the magistrate enforcing the curfew serves someone upstairs). The agent gives the PCs someone to chase while the truth stays buried.

**Incomplete-victory dooms are gold for dark-leaning:** the PCs win the fight but not cleanly, and the threat is *adjourned, not defeated* — a hook into the next arc and a cost that lingers.

## 3. Thematic pillars (3–4)

Derived from the truth — they're what the campaign is *about* emotionally. Each pillar:
- **A short, evocative phrase** — e.g. *"Power always sends the bill"*; *"Every crown is stolen from someone"*; *"Mercy and justice are not the same thing"*; *"No one is only a victim."* (Invent yours from the campaign's truth — don't reuse these.)
- **How it recurs** — the pillar should echo across acts, NPCs, and choices, not appear once.
- **How it touches each PC** — the pillar lands only if it's personal; each character should, at some point, face their own version of it.

Pillars are the GM's **improvisation compass**: when unsure what a scene is "about," return to a pillar. Avoid pillars that are just a setting fact ("magic is dangerous") — a pillar is a *tension or a cost*, something the campaign keeps asking the players.

## 4. Act structure

A skeleton, not a script (the Alexandrian: prep situations, not plots). 3 acts for a campaign (or 3 beats for a single arc). For each:
- **Tone / feel** — how this stretch *feels* (Act I "investigation with creeping wrongness"; Act II "the trap closes"; Act III "open war / contained dread").
- **The turn** — one sentence: *"Act I ends when ___."* The turns are load-bearing — each is where **the truth surfaces another layer**. (E.g., Act I turn: the theft the PCs were hired to solve wasn't theft — the "stolen" things were being *gathered*, by someone who shouldn't still exist.)
- **Level range** — the PF2e levels this act spans.

Keep it lean. The turns are triggers, not scenes; the scenes are built downstream, in play.

## 5. Escalating signs

The world showing the threat **before the PCs understand it** — foreshadowing the GM seeds from session one. Organize **by act, low-frequency to overt.** *(The examples below are a generic horror set — invent your own, themed to your campaign's specific truth, rather than copying these.)*
- **Early** — ambient wrongness no one connects (livestock found bloodless at dawn; a chapel bell that rings with no one in the tower; every dog in town facing the same hill at night).
- **Middle** — the pattern emerges (more fresh graves than there were deaths; a child reciting a name no one taught her; merchants who came through last week have no memory of it).
- **Late** — the veil thins (shadows lagging a beat behind their casters; the river running warm in deep winter; the local priest's prayers coming back wrong).

At tone 3+, signs should **unsettle without explaining** — they're dread, not exposition. They also double as clue-seeds for the revelation map (Section 8 / downstream).

## 6. Open questions

Three buckets (keep them explicit so the GM knows what's decided vs. deliberately not):
- **GM knows, players discover** — truths to reveal in stages. These become the **revelation list / clue-map** (the Alexandrian's three-clue rule: every important truth has at least three independent paths to discovery). Currently downstream/manual — name them so the GM can build the map.
- **Deliberately ambiguous** — the big question left open *on purpose* (was the antagonist right? did the institution have a choice?). Never resolve these for the players; they're the campaign's lingering resonance.
- **Feeds the next arc** — threads left dangling intentionally (where did the survivor flee? was that the only one? who else signed the bargain?).

## 7. Session zero & the players (the collaborative layer)

The piece a pure GM-brainstorm forgets — and a best practice (Sly Flourish, session zero):
- **Worldbuilding questions to bring to players (3–5)** — specific questions whose answers you actually want from the table: their characters' starting bonds, a faction one belongs to, a local detail, "what's something your character lost that they'd cross a line to get back?" Focus on what *you'll actually use*.
- **PC bonds to the theme** — one prompt per pillar tying each character to the campaign's heart, so the theme lands personally rather than as backdrop. (Pillar "Power always sends the bill" → "what did your character gain that they haven't finished paying for?")
- **A safety-tools note** — for dark-leaning play, prompt **Lines & Veils** and an off-ramp (a "pause" signal). Keep it brief and non-preachy: dark themes need buy-in, and naming this up front protects the table without dampening the tone.

## 8. The planning roadmap & handoffs

What makes this skill the **head of the kit**. Emit a layered to-do in working order, routing the rest downstream.

**Locked vs. open** (a real GM document's most useful distinction):
- **Must be locked before play** — the things that break the game if improvised: the antagonist's plan's internal logic, what the relevant deity/power *can and cannot do*, how many key artifacts/macguffins exist and where, the act turns.
- **Can stay open, resolved in play** — the mid-boss's full backstory, secondary NPCs, exact level breaks, how the finale physically looks, consequences of PC choices. (Sly Flourish's spiral: don't pre-build what play will define.)

**Handoffs** — name specific items for each downstream skill:
- → `rpg-region-creator` / `rpg-city-creator`: the places the campaign needs (the base city, the region around the climax site).
- → `rpg-faction-creator`: the antagonist's organization; rival or complicit powers; the institution that buried the truth.
- → `rpg-location-creator`: the climax site, key dungeons, the antagonist's seat.
- → `rpg-npc-creator`: the antagonist, the visible agent/mid-boss, the key allies (and the deity's champion, if any).
- → **clue-map / revelation list** (currently manual GM work; a known future skill): the 8–10 most important truths and the ≥3 places each is discoverable.

## Quick build checklist
1. Central truth stated in 1–2 sentences (what's really going on + the antagonist's arguable motivation + the cost of knowing)?
2. Antagonist presence decided (present/sealed/returning/through-agents) + a plan in motion with an **impending doom**?
3. A visible agent/mid-boss the PCs meet before the true antagonist?
4. 3–4 pillars, each recurring and personal to the PCs?
5. Act turns written as one-sentence triggers that each surface the truth further?
6. Escalating signs by act, unsettling-not-explaining at tone 3 (invented for *this* campaign, not copied)?
7. Open questions sorted into the three buckets (discover / ambiguous / next-arc)?
8. Session-zero questions + PC-bond prompts + a safety note?
9. PF2e stance set (which canon the truth leans on — adopt, don't invent)?
10. Roadmap with locked-vs-open + named handoffs to every relevant downstream skill?
11. Tone held at 3 unless overridden (the villain is arguable; victory costs)?
