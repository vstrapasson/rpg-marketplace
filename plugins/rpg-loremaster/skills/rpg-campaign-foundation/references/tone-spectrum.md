# Tone Spectrum — the 1–5 Dark-Leaning Scale (campaign edition)

Defines the five tone levels this skill can generate. Default is **level 3 (dark-leaning)**. Read this whenever generating at a level other than 3, or whenever unsure how dark a campaign's truth should go. (Mirrors the kit's shared scale, reframed for campaign elements — the truth, the antagonist, the doom, the pillars.)

## The scale

| Level | Label | Reference works | What it feels like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Luminous classical | Dragonlance, classic heroic APs | Clear good vs. evil, a beatable villain, victory expected and earned. |
| **2** | Heroic adventure | Pathfinder default APs, Eberron | Real stakes and grey edges, but heroes can win cleanly. |
| **3 ⭐ default** | **Dark-leaning** | Curse of Strahd, Wrath of the Righteous, Strange Aeons, Witcher 3 | A buried truth, an arguable villain, victories that cost; a campaign that asks hard questions. Light and shadow alternate. |
| **4** | Dark fantasy | Berserk's middle arcs, darker APs | The world is mostly compromised; "winning" means choosing the lesser harm. |
| **5** | Grimdark | Warhammer, ASOIAF at its bleakest | No clean victory; survival and the cost of it are the story. |

## How each level changes the campaign

### Level 1 (Luminous classical)
- The truth is straightforward (a clear evil rises); no inconvenient secret.
- The antagonist is clearly evil; defeating them is unambiguous good.
- Pillars are aspirational (courage, friendship, sacrifice-as-triumph).
- The doom is external; victory is expected and rewarded.

### Level 2 (Heroic adventure)
- The truth has one complication; a grey faction or two.
- The antagonist has an understandable grievance but is still clearly wrong.
- 1 pillar carries real moral weight.
- Victory is likely but not free.

### Level 3 ⭐ (Dark-leaning — DEFAULT)
- **The truth is inconvenient, not just dangerous** — knowing it costs something, and someone buried it for a reason.
- **The antagonist is arguable** — you can state their case as a sympathizer would, and the **"is the villain right?" question is left open** (the GM doesn't confirm or deny).
- **At least one pillar is a moral cost**, not just a value (sacrifice, complicity, the price of knowledge).
- **The doom costs even in victory** — an incomplete-victory finale (the threat adjourned, not destroyed; a price that lingers) is the signature dark-leaning ending.
- **The signs unsettle without explaining**; dread precedes understanding.
- Heroes are absolutely possible — the campaign is winnable — but it asks the players hard questions and charges for the answers. A paladin of Iomedae can be the hero of this campaign *and* be troubled by what it costs.

### Level 4 (Dark fantasy)
- The truth is bleak; the institutions are mostly complicit.
- The antagonist may be *more right than wrong*; opposing them is the lesser evil.
- Most pillars are about cost and compromise.
- The doom is dire; clean victory is off the table.

### Level 5 (Grimdark)
- The truth is an atrocity with no redemptive frame.
- Every power is compromised; there are no clean allies.
- The campaign is about survival and what it costs.
- Add a prominent safety/content advisory; grimdark needs explicit, ongoing buy-in.

## Applying level 3 specifically (the default checklist)
1. **An inconvenient truth** — buried for a reason, costly to know.
2. **An arguable antagonist** — their case is stateable; "is the villain right?" stays open.
3. **A moral-cost pillar** — at least one pillar is a price, not just a virtue.
4. **A costly victory** — the finale charges something; consider an incomplete-victory hook.
5. **Signs that unsettle, not explain.**
6. **A safety note** — dark themes, named up front, protect the table.

The goal of level 3 is **a campaign worth having opinions about**: winnable, heroic at its core, but built on a question that doesn't have a clean answer. Pure light = a campaign that asks nothing. Pure shadow = a campaign that exhausts. Dark-leaning = a campaign that makes the players *choose*, and remember the choice.

## Common mistakes
- **Over-darkening.** If the user asked for 3 and the campaign has no possible clean hero and no winnable arc, that's a 4–5. Pull back: keep heroism possible and victory real, just costly.
- **Under-darkening.** If the truth is just "a bad guy is bad," there's no inconvenient secret, and nothing costs anything, it's a 1–2. Add the buried truth, the arguable motivation, and a price.
- **Edgelord darkness.** Don't pile on atrocity for shock. The darkness must serve the *truth* and the *theme* — the cost of the secret, the price of the cure — not decorate it.

## Sliding between levels
If the user wants a level-3 campaign but a "darker villain", keep the campaign winnable and the pillars heroic while pushing the antagonist's truth toward level 4. Note the deviation so downstream skills (region, faction, location) stay consistent with the intended tone.
