# NPC Frameworks — the design engine

The toolkit for building an NPC as a *person*, not a role. Load this for every substantial NPC. **System-agnostic** — about personality, not stat blocks. Grounded in dramatic-writing craft (want vs. need; the wound), and TTRPG NPC practice (Sly Flourish's "a name, a want, a secret"; the Alexandrian's node-based casts; Robin Laws on dramatic types).

## 1. The core — the engine

The difference between a forgettable NPC and one players talk about for years is **specificity and contradiction**. Real people contain tensions; generic NPCs fail because they're *coherent* in a way real people never are. Build from the inside out — four layers, surface from root:

- **Core belief** — what this person believes about the world that drives their actions ("people are fundamentally selfish"; "power is the only thing that protects you"; "everyone deserves a second chance"). Understandable, but possibly wrong or incomplete.
- **Wound / formative experience** — what happened that created the belief. **Be specific.** Not "a hard childhood" but "her mother sold her to a travelling merchant at seven to clear a gambling debt — and the merchant was kind, which taught her that blood means nothing and strangers can be trusted more than family." The wound must be **active**: it still shapes how they behave, not just their résumé.
- **Want vs. need** — the **want** is the conscious goal they're working toward; the **need** is the thing they actually need but won't admit or can't see. The gap is the drama: a warlord *wants* conquest but *needs* forgiveness; a hermit *wants* isolation but *needs* connection.
- **The contradiction** — where behavior doesn't match stated values, or two parts of them are in genuine tension (the gruff blacksmith who writes poetry; the cheerful innkeeper who poisons people; the ruthless bandit who won't harm children). This gap is what makes players lean in.

**The one-line concept** (put it at the top of the sheet): *"[Name] — [role] who [core contradiction]."*

## 2. Making them playable — the at-the-table layer

Once the internal life is solid, add what a GM needs to *run* them in two seconds:

- **Voice / mannerism** — something performable instantly: a speech pattern (always answers a question with a question), a verbal tic (ends statements with "…but what do I know?"), a physical habit (counts coins when nervous), or an attitude that colours everything. Not a full accent.
- **Appearance** — one or two specific visual details that tell a story, not a full description ("missing two fingers on the left hand, always gloved to hide it").
- **How they react to the PCs** — their default attitude, and what shifts it: what makes them warm, wary, hostile, or afraid.
- **What they know** — the information they hold that's relevant to the campaign (this is where the clue-mapper hooks in).
- **Hooks** — 2–4 ways this person pulls the PCs into a story, emerging from their situation, not bolted on.

## 3. Role-specific patterns

Each role sets the *minimum* the NPC needs; always go past the minimum.

- **Villains / antagonists.** The villain must believe they're right. Give them a motive the players can follow even if they reject the conclusion — the best villains are mirrors of the heroes, wanting similar things by a different path. Ask: *"what would it take for a reasonable person to become this villain?"* If you can't answer, they aren't complex enough. Give them something likable: humour, loyalty, a code, genuine care for something.
- **Quest-givers / patrons.** They need an agenda beyond "please help me, adventurers." *Why can't they solve this themselves? What aren't they telling the PCs? What do they do if the PCs say no?* A patron with a plan B is far more interesting than one who waits.
- **Merchants / service NPCs.** One strong detail that implies a life: the potion-seller who tests every brew on herself and has strange side effects; the smith whose best work has a flaw she can't explain. One detail, one question it raises — enough.
- **Rivals / foils.** A rival should challenge the PCs' *worldview*, not just their swords. Good at something the PCs respect, with a legitimate reason to oppose them. The best rivals are people the PCs wish they could befriend.
- **Mentors / allies.** Memorable when they have limitations, secrets, or agendas: a mentor who's wrong about something important; an ally helping for reasons they won't reveal. Trustworthy NPCs with hidden depth pay off the hardest.

## 4. NPC archetypes (six frames to subvert)

Starting frames — pick one, then **break the mold with at least one thing**. The point of the table is the last column.

| Archetype | Common core belief | Common wound | Default contradiction to give them | Anti-pattern (avoid) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **The Mentor** | "I can guide them past my mistakes" | a student/cause they failed | wise in counsel, manipulating in secret | the flawless sage with no agenda |
| **The Patron / Quest-giver** | "the right people owe the right debts" | a betrayal that cost them everything | generous face, self-serving plan B | the vending machine who just waits |
| **The Merchant / Fixer** | "everything and everyone has a price" | a time price was paid in blood | mercenary surface, one line they won't cross | the quirk with no inner life |
| **The Villain** | "someone has to do what others won't" | a grief that justified the first cruelty | monstrous act, sympathetic logic | cartoon evil with no case |
| **The Rival / Foil** | "I am the better version of them" | being overlooked or second-best | bitter opposition, genuine respect | opposition for opposition's sake |
| **The Loyalist / Guard** | "the order is what keeps us safe" | the time disorder took someone | duty on the surface, doubt underneath | the faceless obstacle |

## 5. Common failure modes

- **The "sad backstory = depth" trap.** Tragedy alone isn't character. What matters is how the past shaped who they are *now* — the coping mechanisms, blind spots, and defenses. Connect every wound to a present behavior.
- **Binary morality.** Avoid pure good or pure evil. Even the cruelest villain needs an understandable motive; even the kindest healer needs a selfish streak. Aim for characters that provoke genuine table debate about whether they're "good."
- **Explaining everything.** Leave gaps. The most intriguing NPCs imply a larger story without spelling it out — a scar they won't discuss, a name they react to, a locked room. Mystery invites engagement.
- **Tropes without subversion.** Starting from an archetype is fine; ending there isn't. Add the one thing that breaks it (§4, last column).

## 6. Coherence & the logic test

Two checks before finalizing. An NPC coheres when **every trait serves the core or the contradiction.**

- **The logic test:** could someone explain *why* this person does what they do, even if they disagree with it? "Greedy" fails; "overcharges because the thieves' guild holds her daughter against a debt" passes. If a trait doesn't trace back to the belief or the wound, it's decoration — cut it or connect it.
- **The sympathizer test:** write one sentence making this person's case as *they* would. If the only case is "they're evil/nice," the character is cardboard — add a true grievance, a real love, or a blind spot.

## Quick build checklist
1. One-line concept stated (name + role + core contradiction)?
2. Core belief named, and a **specific, active** wound behind it?
3. Want and need both named, and in tension?
4. At least one genuine contradiction (surface vs. beneath)?
5. A flaw that creates problems (not a cute quirk)?
6. A performable voice or mannerism (2-second grab)?
7. Logic test passed (someone could explain *why* they act)?
8. Sympathizer test passed (you can argue *for* them) — even for a villain?
9. Gaps and a mystery left open for player curiosity?
10. 2–4 hooks that emerge from who they are, not bolted on (≥1–2 moral-cost at tone 3)?
11. Off-stage handoffs named (their faction → faction-creator; their place → a location-creator)?
